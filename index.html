Write Up

Our project began with a simple question: Are wildfires in the United States just becoming more common, or are they also becoming more intense? Using NASA MODIS satellite wildfire data, we wanted to explore how wildfire behavior has changed across different regions of the country over time, not just through the number of fires detected, but also through their severity and intensity.

At first, we tried visualizing every wildfire detection as individual points on a map. While this showed the scale of the dataset, it became way too overplotted. Thousands of overlapping points made it difficult to distinguish meaningful regional patterns or long-term trends. Instead of helping users understand the data, the visualization became cluttered and hard to interpret. Due to this, we shifted our approach toward a more interactive and aggregated design. We created a dashboard centered around a U.S. choropleth map displaying wildfire activity from 2001 to 2025. Users can switch between two perspectives of the data: Fire Count and Average Intensity (FRP). We used separate color scales, red for wildfire frequency and blue for intensity, to make the distinction visually clear and easier to compare across states.

As we developed the project further, we realized that fire counts alone did not fully represent wildfire severity. Some regions appeared relatively stable in terms of total fire detections, but when we analyzed FRP and brightness values, we saw much stronger increases in fire intensity. This became especially noticeable when comparing western states to parts of the American South. FRP was particularly important because it measures the amount of radiative energy released by a fire, making it a strong indicator of wildfire intensity.

To make the visualization more exploratory, we added interactive features throughout the dashboard. Hovering over a state reveals information such as total detections, average FRP, and maximum FRP values for that year. Clicking on a state opens a drill-down view where users can analyze wildfire activity within that region in more detail. Instead of displaying every wildfire point individually in the state view, we grouped detections into clustered regions to reduce overplotting and improve readability. Users can then use drag-based brushing to select specific areas within a state and compare regional wildfire behavior over time. The visualization also categorizes fire count and intensity into three cluster levels (10, 20, and 30) making patterns easier to identify visually.

Overall, the project evolved from a simple wildfire map into an interactive system for exploring how wildfire frequency and intensity change across both geography and time.

Visualization Design Decisions

National Choropleth Map

Our main visualization is an interactive U.S. choropleth map showing wildfire activity by state. Users can switch between:

  • Fire Count
  • Average Fire Intensity (FRP)

We included both because fire counts alone do not fully represent wildfire severity. Some states may have fewer fires but much higher intensity levels. We used a binned color scale instead of a continuous gradient to improve readability and make regional differences easier to distinguish.

The map includes:

  • Year slider for temporal exploration
  • Zoom and pan
  • Hovering over each state
  • Clicking on each state to show the firing rate/Intensity of that area

Hovering over a state reveals:

  • Fire count
  • Average FRP
  • Maximum FRP

The side panel also displays national summary statistics such as total detections, peak state, average FRP, and maximum recorded FRP.

State Drill-Down View

Clicking a state opens a detailed regional view showing clustered wildfire detections within that state. We originally considered plotting every wildfire point individually, but this caused heavy overplotting. Instead, we used clustered circles to better show spatial density and regional hotspots while keeping the visualization readable. Circle size represents wildfire concentration across locations in three bins: 10, 20 and 30+.

Brushing Interaction

The drill-down map supports brushing, allowing users to drag over specific regions within a state. Once a region is brushed, the side panel dynamically updates to show trends only for the selected area. This allows users to compare localized wildfire behavior within the same state. We chose brushing because it supports exploratory analysis and reveals regional differences that state-level summaries alone cannot show. We also users to be able to get the average trends over a bigger area and not just a point of wildfires in each region/state.

Temporal Trend Graphs

The side panel contains two linked trend graphs:

  • Intensity Trend (Average FRP % Change): Shows how wildfire intensity changes over time using percentage change in FRP values. This graph highlights that wildfire intensity can increase even when fire counts remain relatively stable.
  • Frequency Trend (Raw Fire Count): Shows total wildfire detections over time for the selected state or brushed region.

Displaying both graphs together allows users to compare wildfire frequency versus severity directly.

Visual Styling and Layout

We used a dark-themed interface to improve contrast and emphasize wildfire-related colors such as orange and red for the firing rate and blue and white for the average intensity.

The dashboard layout keeps:

  • The map as the main focus,
  • Controls easily accessible,
  • Trend graphs and statistics visible during interaction.

We also redesigned the layout multiple times to create a more connected dashboard experience where all views update together dynamically.

Why These Interactions Matter

Wildfire data is both spatial and temporal, making interaction essential.

Our visualization allows users to:

  • Explore national wildfire patterns,
  • Drill into specific states,
  • Brush localized regions,
  • Compare fire counts and intensity trends over time.

This layered interaction design helps users uncover patterns that would be difficult to identify in a static visualization.

Development Process

Our team divided responsibilities across:

  • Data cleaning and preprocessing
  • D3 visualization implementation
  • Interaction and filtering logic
  • Styling and layout design
  • Narrative writeup and interpretation

The development process involved extensive experimentation with both visual encodings and interaction techniques. One of the most time consuming aspects was handling the scale of the MODIS dataset while maintaining smooth interactivity in the browser. We spent significant time optimizing rendering performance and restructuring data aggregation pipelines. Another major challenge was determining how to visually encode wildfire intensity. Early versions focused too heavily on fire counts, which masked important differences in severity. Incorporating FRP and brightness measurements significantly improved the analytical depth of the project. In total, we estimate spending approximately 40 hours developing the application, including data preprocessing, debugging, design iteration, and deployment.
